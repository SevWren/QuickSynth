**1. "TL;DR First" Version**
Optimized for the scroll-and-skim reader who decides *then* dives deeper.
```
Read the article from [URL] and produce a structured briefing for a general audience:

**TL;DR** (1 sentence max): What happened, and why does it matter?

**Key Facts** (3-5 bullets): Who, what, when, where — plain language only.

**Why It Matters**: 2-3 sentences on real-world impact for everyday people.

**Fine Print** (collapsible-style header): Any caveats, scope limits, or missing context the reader should know.

Rules: No jargon. If a term needs explaining, define it inline in parentheses. Bold proper nouns and numbers.
```

---

**2. "Confidence Levels" Version**
For the skeptical reader who wants to know what's confirmed vs. speculated.
```
Analyze the article at [URL] and summarize it for a non-expert reader using this structure:

- **Headline** (one plain-English sentence stating the core event)
- **Confirmed Facts** ✅: Bullet points for things explicitly stated in the article
- **Unclear / Alleged** ⚠️: Bullet points for claims, speculation, or unverified details
- **Background You Need**: 1-2 sentences of context — assume the reader hasn't followed this story
- **The Bigger Picture**: One sentence on why this story exists in the news cycle

Keep language at a 8th-grade reading level. Flag loaded or political language with [Note: charged term].
```

---

**3. "News Card" Version**
Mimics how apps like Apple News or Artifact present stories — clean and skimmable.
```
Summarize the article at [URL] as a "news card" for a busy adult with no prior knowledge of the topic:

**[TOPIC TAG]** — e.g., Politics | Economy | Health

**What's Happening** (2 sentences max)
**Who's Involved** (named people/groups, one descriptor each)
**What Changed or Was Decided**
**What Happens Next** (if stated)
**Read More If**: [one sentence on who should care most about this story]

Avoid editorializing. Use the article's own language for quotes, but paraphrase everything else simply.
```

---

**4. "5 Questions" Version**
Uses a journalistic framework everyday readers already intuitively understand.
```
Using the article at [URL], answer only the questions the article actually addresses. Skip any it doesn't answer.

1. **What happened?** (1-2 sentences)
2. **Who is involved, and what are their roles?**
3. **Why did this happen / what led to it?**
4. **What are the consequences or next steps?**
5. **What is contested, uncertain, or left out?**

Format: Bold the question, plain-paragraph answer. Flag speculation vs. confirmed fact. 
Final line: *Source reliability note* — briefly state what type of outlet this is (e.g., wire service, partisan outlet, trade publication).
```

---

**5. "Layered Depth" Version**
Best overall for your use case — gives the quick hit *and* a drilldown path.
```
Analyze the article at [URL] and produce a three-layer summary for a general reader:

**Layer 1 — Headline Brief** (≤25 words): The single most important thing to know.

**Layer 2 — Core Summary** (3-5 bullets): Key facts, named parties, outcomes, and timeline. 
  - Bold numbers, names, and dates. 
  - Flag any significant caveats with ⚠️

**Layer 3 — Full Context** (short paragraph): Background, implications, and what questions remain unanswered. 
  - *Italicize* speculative or opinion-based content.

Audience: Assume a smart adult with no prior knowledge of this story. 
No jargon without inline definition. No filler phrases like "It's worth noting."
```

Here are 5 more, pushing into different design philosophies:

---

**6. "Bias Awareness" Version**
For readers who want the facts *and* a media literacy lens baked in.
```
Analyze the article at [URL] and produce a summary for a general reader that includes editorial transparency:

**The Claim**: What is the article asserting? (1 sentence)
**The Evidence**: What facts, sources, or data does it use to support that claim?
**What's Missing**: What perspective, data, or voices are absent?
**Loaded Language Alert**: List any emotionally charged words or framing used — quote them directly.
**Outlet Context**: Based on the URL, note the publication type (e.g., cable news affiliate, wire service, opinion platform).

Summary tone: Neutral. Do not adopt the article's framing. 
Use plain language. Bullet points preferred over paragraphs.
```

---

**7. "Before & After" Version**
Ideal for ongoing stories — anchors the reader in what changed, not just what happened.
```
Read the article at [URL] and structure your summary around *change*:

**Status Before**: What was the situation before this event/decision/announcement?
**What Changed**: The specific new development (facts only, 2-3 bullets)
**Immediate Reaction**: How have people, institutions, or markets responded (if stated)?
**What's Still the Same**: What remains unresolved or unchanged?
**Timeline Anchor**: When did this happen, and where does it fit in the broader story?

Assume the reader has heard the topic name but knows nothing else.
Bold all dates, names, and figures. One sentence of jargon-free context for any technical term.
```

---

**8. "Explain It to a Friend" Version**
Conversational tone — reads like a knowledgeable friend texting you a summary.
```
Read the article at [URL]. Summarize it the way a well-informed friend would explain it over 
coffee — clear, honest, and without condescension. Use this loose structure:

- Open with: "So basically, what happened is..." (2-3 sentences of plain-English context)
- Follow with: "The key things to know are..." (3-4 punchy bullets)
- End with: "The part that's still unclear / worth watching is..." (1-2 sentences)

Rules: 
- No bullet should exceed 20 words.
- Spell out any acronym on first use.
- If the story has a villain, victim, or winner — name them plainly without editorializing.
- Do not start any sentence with "It is important to note."
```

---

**9. "Decision-Maker" Version**
For readers who want to know: *does this affect me, and what should I do with this information?*
```
Summarize the article at [URL] for a general adult reader who wants to know if this story 
is relevant to their life. Use this structure:

**The Core Event** (1 sentence): Strip it to its simplest form.
**Who Is Directly Affected**: List specific groups, regions, or demographics mentioned.
**Who Should Pay Attention**: Plain-language explanation of downstream effects on ordinary people.
**Action or Watch Items**: Is there anything a reader might want to track, verify, or act on?
**Credibility Check**: Are named sources identified? Is evidence cited or asserted?

Tone: Practical, not alarming. Avoid passive voice. 
If the article is opinion or analysis (not straight news), say so upfront in bold.
```

---

**10. "Structured Debate" Version**
Best for politically charged or contested topics — surfaces all sides without picking one.
```
Analyze the article at [URL] and present a balanced summary for a reader who wants to 
understand all sides before forming an opinion:

**The Issue in Plain Terms**: What is the core dispute or event? (2 sentences max)

**Side A argues**: Bullet the strongest points made by one perspective
**Side B argues**: Bullet the strongest points made by the opposing perspective
**Where Both Sides Agree** (if anywhere): Any common ground stated or implied
**What the Article Doesn't Tell You**: Gaps, missing voices, or unasked questions

Ground rules for your response:
- Give equal word count to each side.
- Do not signal which side is correct.
- *Italicize* any claims that are opinions, predictions, or allegations rather than verified facts.
- Final line must be: "For full context, read the original source."
```
